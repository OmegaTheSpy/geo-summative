About Edmonton

Edmonton is the capital of the western Canadian province of Alberta. It is the 6th largest city in terms of population (as of 2011) with a population of 1,159,869.

Land Form Regions

There are seven distinct landform regions in canada but they can be categorized as shield, lowlands and highlands. The landform region in the shield category is the Canadian Shield. It is the largest of the landform regions ,covering more than half (4’800’000km2) of Canada, and it is the oldest. The landform regions in the lowlands are Hudson's Bay-Arctic Lowlands, Great Lakes and St. Lawrence lowlands and the Interior Plains. They are categorized by being flat, no mountains and low terrain. The regions categorized as Highlands are Innuitian Mountains, Western Cordelia and Appellations (welcome to). They are categorized by not being flat and having mountains.
Edmonton is located in the interior plains. The interior plains stretch from the arctic ocean to the 49th parallel. During formation they were covered by shallow seas and sediment was deposited there from the rocky mountains. After millions of years the sediment compressed under the weight of the newer layers. This formed sedimentary rock. One of the layers contained coral reefs this formed much of the oil and gas that is found in alberta and saskatchewan along with other smaller pockets of minerals.

Climate

As Canada is such a large country with many landform regions the climate varies a lot across the country. It also has two types of climate, continental and maritime. This also adds more variety to the climate across the country because of the moderating effect in maritime climates. The size of the country also has an effect because as you approach the north pole it gets colder.

Canadian Identity

Canada’s identity is very heavily based on its environment and landscape. Much of our history is based off of the land. When People first arrived in Canada they had to live off the land. Many of the industries Canada is known for relies on the land. This reliance on the land in Canada’s history and industries shaped what Canada is known for.

Natural disasters in Canada

In Canada there are a variety of natural disasters, because we are such a large country. Depending on where you are you may be affected by very different natural disasters. Some of the natural disasters include:

Earthquakes (along fault lines on the west coast)
Floods (everywhere)
Hail(anywhere if cold enough)
Wildfires(in dry areas)
Icebergs, Sea Ice, Fog (along the coast)
Landslides, Avalanches(anywhere without warning)
Tornadoes(south western Ontario and southern prairies)
Blizzards, Tsunamis, Storm Surges(coast)

We are also affected by natural disasters that occur in other parts of the the world, because we are such a large country. As an example of this, during the aftermath of the 2011 tsunami in Japan tons of debris washed up on the shore of BC. Another example is when a volcano erupted in alaska and it delayed planes in Canada.

Waste Management

Edmonton has a state of the art waste management system. Everything Starts off at the Integrated Process and Transfer Facility where it is separated into groups for each process. At the Waste to Biofuels and Chemicals Facility 140’000 tonnes of municipal solid waste is turned in to 38 million liters of biofuel each year. The biofuel is better for the environment. The Organics Processing Program (composting) takes 160’000 tonnes of organic material and turns it into compost. The Materials Recovery Facility can process 70’000 tonnes of recyclable materials per year. The recyclable materials are gathered from homes and apartments via the blue bag(for houses) and blue bin (for larger buildings) system. These three facilities divert more than 50% of Edmonton’s waste from landfills. The remaining waste is put into landfills but it does not just sit there, the city of Edmonton uses a gas recovery method to harness the power of the methane and carbon dioxide. The power generated from this method is used to power 4’500 houses (1% of the total households) yearly.

Why Is This Important?
Waste management is important because Canada wants to preserve its environment, as stated previously Canada cares a lot about about its environment as it has shaped its identity. Having a good environment can also improve the health of people living there.

Immigration process:
Federal Skilled Worker Class

One of the ways to immigrate to Canada is to apply under the Federal Skilled Worker class. When talking about the Federal Skilled Worker or (more broadly) the Economic Class Canada is looking for someone who can become financially stable in Canada. To become a permanent resident of Canada under this class you must first enter the express entry pool. Then you are given a score by CIC. CIC determines your score with a points based system that involves six criteria:

English and/or French skills
Education
Experience
Age
Arranged employment in Canada
Adaptability

You must be able to show proof that you are telling the truth in all of these areas. You must score 67 (out of 100) points or higher to be considered/qualify for this immigration class. If you are one of the top candidates in the pool you are then invited to become a permanent resident of Canada. To then become a citizen you must have lived in Canada for at least 4 years then you must take a test that costs $100.
